We had to do a small user study for which I asked friends and my family. They all got time to get used to the game, and then they performed a whole run where I tracked how they are playing. I divided the study into two parts. The first part is based on the game mechanics: how easy it was to understand, how well they performed on the two skill-based minigames (reaction and T-shape), and how much fun. The second part is based on the VR-implementation: motion sickness, presence, and fatigue. In the end, the participants could give some feedback.

My mother tried to play the game, but since she normally has a problem with dizziness, she got so much motion sickness after the first jump that she couldn't play anymore.

**Part 1**

As you can see in the first figure, the game mechanics were easy to understand. The fun is more diverse. The game mechanics themselves were good, but the motivation was too low, since there was no real reward. You can compare with others or your previous run, how fast you were, or how many minigames you had to play, but the random factor is too big to really compare with.

{{< figure src="images/part_1_1.png" caption="Here are the results of part 1." >}}

Since the number of times the participants got the reaction game, I took the average. You can see that in the T-shape interaction, the average value is higher than in the reaction game. But as I wished, the result shows that they both are better than a random dice which has an expected value of 3.5.

{{< figure src="images/part_1_2.png" caption="Here are the results of the two skill-based minigames." >}}

**Part 2**

Motion sickness is very different among the players. It's mostly like the player said beforehand, whether he gets easily motion sick or not. Fatigue is due to motion sickness or due to the flickering of the world. For me, in one game, I don't have fatigue, but as the programmer, I have to play it multiple times, and then I still don't have motion sickness, but a lot of fatigue after two or three games. Presence is relatively good, but it is not interesting enough to completely captivate the players.

{{< figure src="images/part_2.png" caption="Here are the results of part 2." >}}

**Feedback**

 - The idea is good, but with more skill-based minigames, it would be better.
 - More motivation to play the game more often. (Rankings with the amount of rolls needed, or stats from the minigames.)
 - Place the objects at a height based on the height of the headset.
 - The objects are too big.
 - Give an acoustic or visible timer for the reaction game.
 - The world is flickering too much.
